Glossary

The canonical language for this course. Every lesson uses these terms; they're added once you can use them correctly.

Last updated: Lesson D2•Deep-linked from every lesson

AForces & Efficiency

LiftL

The aerodynamic force perpendicular to the oncoming airflow. It holds the aircraft up by deflecting air downward (Newton) — the same event as the low pressure over the wing's upper surface (Bernoulli).

Avoid: “suck”, “the wing pushing up on air”.

DragD

The aerodynamic force parallel to the airflow, opposing motion. The propulsion system spends energy to overcome it; less drag for the same lift means longer flight.

Lift coefficientCL

A dimensionless number capturing how much lift a wing makes for its size and speed, stripped of air density, area and velocity. Rises with angle of attack until stall.

Avoid: “lift number”, “lift factor”.

Drag coefficientCD

The dimensionless counterpart for drag. For a whole wing it splits into a parasitic (friction + pressure) part and an induced (price-of-lift) part.

Lift-to-drag ratioL/D = CL/CD

The master efficiency number: lift produced per unit of drag paid. Maximum L/D gives the flattest glide and the best range. A 1–2 m model wing peaks around L/D ≈ 12–18.

Avoid: “glide number” — glide ratio is a consequence of L/D, not a design synonym.

Angle of attackα (alpha)

The angle between the wing's chord line and the oncoming airflow — the pilot/autopilot's main lever on CL. Too high → stall.

Avoid: “pitch angle” — pitch is relative to the ground, not the airflow.

BEndurance (the north star)

Endurance

Time aloft. For a battery-electric drone, maximised by minimising power required — distinct from range, which minimises drag-per-distance.

Endurance parameterCL1.5/CD

The quantity a propeller aircraft must maximise for longest flight time. Because it weights lift more heavily than L/D does, peak endurance happens slower and at a higher CL than peak L/D. The recurring target of this whole course.

Avoid: confusing with L/D — they peak at different speeds.

Drag polar

A plot of CL (vertical) against CD (horizontal) as angle of attack sweeps. The single most useful chart in wing design; every “best” operating point is a geometric construction on it. Modelled as CD = CD0 + CL²/(π·AR·e).

CMaking Lift

Flow turning

The correct account of lift: a wing deflects air downward, and the reaction pushes it up (Newton). The curved streamlines also imply lower pressure above (Bernoulli) — one event, two descriptions.

Avoid: “equal transit time”, “air catches up at the trailing edge” — a debunked myth.

Lift curve

The plot of CL versus angle of attack. Linear between the zero-lift angle and stall, with slope ≈ 0.11 per degree (≈ 2π per radian) for a thin airfoil.

Zero-lift angleα0

The angle of attack at which a wing makes no lift. Negative for a cambered airfoil (ours ≈ −2°).

Stall

The breakdown of lift when angle of attack exceeds a critical value (≈ 12° for ours) and the boundary-layer flow separates. CL collapses and drag spikes; sets the minimum flight speed.

Avoid: “the engine stalling” (unrelated).

Camber

The curvature of an airfoil's mean line. More camber shifts the whole lift curve up (more CL at a given angle) — useful for the high-lift, slow flight endurance wants.

DDrag & Planform

Parasitic dragCD0

The lift-independent drag floor: skin friction plus pressure (wake) drag of the shape. Roughly constant in coefficient form at a given Reynolds number; dominates at low CL (fast flight).

Avoid: “form drag” — that's only the pressure part.

Induced dragCDi = CL²/(π·AR·e)

The drag created specifically by making lift on a finite-span wing. Grows with the square of lift; dominates at high CL (slow flight) and is ~75% of total drag at the endurance point.

Avoid: “drag due to lift” (correct but verbose), “vortex drag”.

Aspect ratioAR = b²/S

How long and slender a wing is — span (b) squared over area (S). Higher AR means lower induced drag, hence longer endurance. The master planform lever, traded against weight and Reynolds number.

Span efficiencye

How close a wing's lift distribution is to the ideal elliptical one (e = 1). Typically ≈ 0.85; appears in the denominator of induced drag.

Avoid: “Oswald efficiency” — technically the whole-aircraft version.

Downwash / tip vortex

Air leaking around the wingtips rolls into two trailing vortices, which drive a downward flow (downwash) over the wing. The downwash tilts the lift vector backward — that backward component is induced drag.

EReynolds & the Low-Re Regime

Reynolds numberRe = ρVc/μ = Vc/ν

The ratio of inertial to viscous forces in a flow, based on chord c and airspeed V (ν ≈ 1.5×10⁻⁵ m²/s for air). A 1–2 m drone flies at Re ≈ 100k–400k — low enough that viscosity dominates and many full-scale airfoils misbehave.

Avoid: treating it as a property of the airfoil — it's a property of the flow condition.

Boundary layer

The thin layer of slowed air clinging to the wing's surface, where all skin-friction drag is generated. It begins laminar (smooth) and may transition to turbulent (mixed) further back.

Laminar separation bubble

At low Reynolds number the laminar boundary layer separates before it can transition, then sometimes re-attaches — trapping a “bubble” that adds drag and can trigger early stall. The defining headache of low-Re airfoil design.

Transition

The change of the boundary layer from laminar to turbulent. Its location sets drag; XFOIL models it with the Ncrit parameter, and low-Re CFD needs a dedicated transition model.

FAirfoil Geometry

Chord line

The straight line from the leading edge to the trailing edge. Its length is the chord (c); angle of attack is measured from it.

Mean camber line

The line halfway between the upper and lower surfaces. Its curvature is the airfoil's camber; a symmetric airfoil's mean line coincides with the chord line.

Thickness

The distance between upper and lower surfaces, given as a % of chord at its maximum. More thickness softens the stall and adds structural room, but raises drag — especially at low Reynolds number.

NACA 4-digit codeMPXX

A compact airfoil parametrization: M = max camber (% chord), P = its position (tenths of chord), XX = max thickness (% chord). E.g. NACA 2412 = 2% camber at 0.4c, 12% thick.

Drag bucket

The low-drag trough in an airfoil's polar over a range of lift coefficients where the flow stays favourable. A foil whose bucket spans your design CL is efficient where it matters.

Design lift coefficient

The CL the wing is intended to cruise at — for us, the endurance value (~1.0–1.1). Airfoil and planform choices are tuned so this CL lands in the drag bucket and near peak CL1.5/CD.

GPlanform & Sizing

Wing loadingW/S

Weight per unit wing area. Lower wing loading means slower flight at a given lift coefficient (V ∝ √(W/S)) — endurance wants it low, traded against bigger, floppier wings. Often quoted in g/dm² by modellers.

Stall speedVstall

The minimum flyable speed, where the wing reaches CLmax: Vstall = √(2W/(ρ·S·CLmax)). Cruise speed must keep a comfortable margin above it.

Planform

The wing's shape seen from above — span, chord distribution, taper and sweep. Sets aspect ratio and, through the lift distribution, span efficiency.

Taper ratioλ = ctip/croot

How much the chord shrinks from root to tip. A moderate taper (λ ≈ 0.4) approximates the ideal elliptical loading cheaply, pushing e toward 1.

Elliptical loading

The spanwise lift distribution that minimises induced drag (span efficiency e = 1). The benchmark every real planform is measured against; taper and twist are how you approach it.

Lifting-line theory

Prandtl's classic model of a finite wing as a line of vortices, predicting the lift distribution, induced drag and reduced lift-curve slope. The method behind XFLR5's fast wing analysis.

Vortex-lattice methodVLM

A panel method that tiles the wing with horseshoe vortices to compute 3-D lift and induced drag for arbitrary planforms — XFLR5's option when lifting-line assumptions break down.

HTools & CFD

XFOIL

Mark Drela's 2-D airfoil code (MIT): an inviscid panel method coupled to a viscous boundary-layer solver. Fast and well-validated for low-Re section polars — the workhorse of this course.

Panel method

A solver that represents a surface with many small vortex/source panels and solves the inviscid flow around them. Cheap and accurate for attached flow; it cannot model heavy separation on its own.

Ncrit

XFOIL's eN transition setting: ≈ 9 for clean, low-turbulence air; ≈ 7 for average free flight; lower for turbulent/rough conditions. Lower Ncrit = earlier transition = more drag.

Viscous polar

A polar computed with viscosity (in XFOIL, after setting VISC <Re>), so it includes real drag. An inviscid run gives lift but essentially no drag and is useless for our purposes.

CFD

Computational fluid dynamics: solving the flow equations on a 3-D mesh (e.g. OpenFOAM, SU2). Higher fidelity than panel methods for separated/3-D flow, at much greater setup and compute cost.

RANS

Reynolds-Averaged Navier–Stokes — the standard, affordable CFD approach that models turbulence statistically. The usual first rung of real CFD for wings.

Transition model

An add-on (e.g. γ-Reθ / Langtry–Menter) that lets RANS predict where the boundary layer trips turbulent. Essential at low Re — without it, CFD over-predicts drag badly.

Avoid: assuming default “fully turbulent” RANS is valid for our regime.

Mesh

The grid of cells a CFD solver computes on. Results must be shown mesh-independent (unchanged as the mesh is refined) before they're trusted.
