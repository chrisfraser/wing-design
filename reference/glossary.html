Forces & Efficiency

LiftL

The aerodynamic force perpendicular to the oncoming airflow. It holds the aircraft up by deflecting air downward (Newton) — which is the same event as the low pressure on the upper surface (Bernoulli).

Avoid: "suck", "the wing pushing up on air"

DragD

The aerodynamic force parallel to the airflow, opposing motion. The engine must spend energy to overcome it; less drag for the same lift means longer flight.

Lift coefficientCL

A dimensionless number capturing how much lift a wing makes for its size and speed, stripped of air density, area, and velocity. Rises with angle of attack until stall.

Avoid: "lift number", "lift factor"

Drag coefficientCD

The dimensionless counterpart for drag. For a whole wing it splits into parasitic (friction + shape) and induced (the price of making lift) parts.

Lift-to-drag ratioL/D = CL/CD

The master efficiency number: lift produced per unit of drag paid. Maximum L/D gives the flattest glide and the best range. A 1–2 m model wing peaks around L/D ≈ 10–20.

Avoid: "glide number" (glide ratio is a consequence of L/D, not a synonym in design)

Angle of attackα (alpha)

The angle between the wing's chord line and the oncoming airflow. The pilot/autopilot's main lever on CL. Too high → stall.

Avoid: "pitch angle" (pitch is relative to the ground, not the airflow)

Endurance (our north star)

Endurance

Time aloft. For our battery-electric drone, maximised by minimising power required — distinct from range, which minimises drag-per-distance.

Endurance parameterCL1.5/CD

The quantity a propeller aircraft must maximise for longest flight time. Because it weights lift more heavily than L/D does, peak endurance happens slower and at a higher CL than peak L/D. The recurring target of this whole course.

Avoid: confusing with L/D — they peak at different speeds.

Making Lift

Flow turning

The correct account of lift: a wing deflects air downward, and the reaction pushes it up (Newton). The curved streamlines also imply lower pressure above (Bernoulli) — one event, two descriptions.

Avoid: "equal transit time", "air catches up at the trailing edge" (a debunked myth)

Lift curve

The plot of CL versus angle of attack. Linear between the zero-lift angle and stall, with slope ≈ 0.11 per degree (≈ 2π per radian) for a thin airfoil.

Zero-lift angleα0

The angle of attack at which a wing makes no lift. Negative for a cambered airfoil (ours ≈ −2°).

Stall

The breakdown of lift when the angle of attack exceeds a critical value (≈ 12° for ours) and the boundary-layer flow separates. CL collapses and drag spikes. Sets the minimum flight speed.

Avoid: "the engine stalling" (unrelated)

Camber

The curvature of an airfoil's mean line. More camber shifts the whole lift curve up (more CL at a given angle) — useful for the high-lift, slow flight endurance wants.

Drag polar

A plot of CL (vertical) against CD (horizontal) as angle of attack sweeps. The single most useful chart in airfoil/wing design — every "best" operating point is a geometric construction on this curve.
