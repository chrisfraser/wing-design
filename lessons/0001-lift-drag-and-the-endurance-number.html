Lift, Drag & the Number That Decides Endurance

Before we can design a wing, we need a single, measurable definition of “better.” This lesson hands you that number — and the surprise hiding inside it.

⏱ ~12 minutes• 🧮 Light math — concepts first• Starts the course · no prerequisites
◎ Why this lesson

Your goal is a 1–2 m battery-electric drone that stays up as long as possible. “Make it efficient” is useless until it's a number you can compute and compare. By the end of this lesson you'll own that number — and you'll have discovered that the setting for maximum endurance is not the one most people would guess. It's the north star the whole course steers by.

A wing is a machine for trading air into an upward force. It is never free: every gram of lift comes with a tax called drag, and your battery pays that tax for the entire flight. The whole game of endurance is buying the most lift for the least drag.

1Two forces, made comparable

A wing produces lift (perpendicular to the airflow, holding you up) and drag (along the airflow, holding you back). But raw force in newtons can't compare two wings — it depends on how fast you fly, how dense the air is, and how big the wing is. Engineers strip those out into dimensionless coefficients:1

L = ½ · ρ · V² · S · CL D = ½ · ρ · V² · S · CD ρ = air density, V = airspeed, S = wing area. The coefficients CL and CD are the wing's “character” — independent of how fast or how big it is.

2The master number: lift-to-drag ratio

Divide one by the other and the messy physics — density, speed, area — cancels clean away:

L/D = CL / CD Lift produced per unit of drag paid — the headline efficiency of any wing. A good 1–2 m model wing reaches L/D ≈ 12–18. Maximum L/D gives the flattest glide and the best range (distance).

Two wings with the same L/D are equally efficient, full stop — which is exactly why this single number is where wing design begins.

3The lever: angle of attack

You don't change a wing's coefficients by wishing — you change them with the angle of attack (α), the tilt between the wing and the oncoming air. Raise α and CL climbs… but CD climbs faster, because drag grows roughly with lift squared. So L/D rises, peaks, then falls. Somewhere sits a single sweet-spot angle — let's find it.

4The surprise: endurance is not max L/D

Switch the explorer to Goal B. The best point jumps up and to the right — to a higher angle, a higher CL, and a slower flight speed. Why? Staying aloft longest isn't about minimum drag; it's about minimum power, and power is drag × speed. Flying slower cuts the power bill even though drag-per-lift is slightly worse.2

maximise CL1.5 / CD → longest flight time For a propeller aircraft, endurance peaks at maximum CL1.5/CD (minimum power), while range peaks at maximum L/D (minimum drag-per-distance). They are different points on the same polar.
★ Your north star

From here on, every airfoil, every planform, every trade-off is judged against one quantity: CL1.5/CD. Range wants max L/D; your mission — time aloft — wants max CL1.5/CD, which means flying slower and at a higher lift coefficient than a range-optimised aircraft would.

What you can now say

  1. Lift and drag are turned into dimensionless CL and CD so any two wings compare on equal terms.
  2. L/D = CL/CD is the master efficiency number; max L/D gives the best range.
  3. Angle of attack is the lever that sweeps CL and CD along the drag polar.
  4. For a propeller aircraft, endurance peaks at max CL1.5/CD — slower and higher-lift than max L/D.
  5. That quantity is the course's north star: every later choice is judged by it.
