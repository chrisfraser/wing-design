Lift, Drag & the Number That Decides Endurance

What does it actually mean for a wing to be “optimal”?

Why this, first
Your goal is a 1–2 m battery-electric drone that stays up as long as possible. Before we can design a wing, we need a single, measurable definition of “better.” This lesson hands you that number — and shows you the surprise hiding inside it.

A wing is a machine for trading air into an upward force. It is never free: every gram of lift comes with a tax called drag, and your battery pays that tax the entire flight. The whole game of endurance is buying the most lift for the least drag.

Two forces, made comparable

A wing produces lift (perpendicular to the airflow, holding you up) and drag (along the airflow, holding you back). Lift exists because the wing turns air downward; by Newton's third law the air pushes the wing up — the very same event as the lower pressure over the wing's top surface.If you've heard “air travels farther over the top so it must go faster” — that's the equal-transit-time myth, and it's wrong. NASA Glenn debunks it here. We'll get the real story in Lesson 2.

Raw lift in newtons isn't useful for comparing wings — it depends on how fast you fly, how dense the air is, and how big the wing is. Engineers strip those out to get dimensionless coefficients:

L = ½ ρ V2 S CL      D = ½ ρ V2 S CD

ρ = air density, V = airspeed, S = wing area. The coefficients CL and CD are the wing's “character” — independent of how fast or how big. Two wings with the same CL/CD are equally efficient, period.

The master number

Divide them and the messy physics cancels: L/D = CL / CD — lift produced per unit of drag paid. This is the headline efficiency of any wing. A good 1–2 m model wing reaches L/D ≈ 12–18. Maximum L/D gives the flattest glide and the best range.

The lever: angle of attack

You don't change a wing's coefficients by wishing — you change them with the angle of attack (α), the tilt between the wing and the oncoming air. Raise α and CL climbs… but CD climbs faster, because drag grows roughly with lift squared. So L/D rises, peaks, then falls. Somewhere there is a single sweet-spot angle. Let's go find it.

The surprise: endurance is not max L/D

Switch the simulator to Goal B. Notice the best point moves up and to the right — to a higher angle of attack, a higher CL, and a slower flight speed. That's because staying aloft longest isn't about minimum drag; it's about minimum power, and power is drag × speed. Flying slower cuts the power bill even though drag-per-lift is slightly worse.

Your north star

For a propeller aircraft, endurance is maximised at maximum CL1.5/CD, not maximum L/D. (Range = max L/D; endurance = max CL1.5/CD. Derived in Marchman Ch. 6.) Every airfoil and wing choice from here on will be judged against this one quantity.

Why do engineers use CL and CD instead of raw lift and drag in newtons?

The coefficients are dimensionless: they factor out air density, airspeed and wing area, so two different wings can be compared on equal terms.
