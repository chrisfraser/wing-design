Planform & Wing Loading

Coefficients are abstract. This lesson turns them into the four numbers you can actually measure with a tape and a scale: span, chord, speed — and the Reynolds number they force.

⏱ ~14 minutes• 🧮 Real numbers — keep a calculator handy• Builds on F3 · F4
◎ Why this lesson

In T1 you generated your own airfoil polars — but a polar is still just character: dimensionless CL and CD that describe a slice of wing, not a wing. Before the capstone you need to commit to real geometry and a real speed. This lesson does the algebra that converts your endurance lift coefficient (from F1) and your aspect-ratio ambition (from F3) into a span, a chord, a cruise speed — and the Reynolds number (F4) that chord lands you in. Those numbers are the raw material the capstone D1 assembles.

Pick a weight and a wing area and you have, whether you meant to or not, already chosen how fast your aircraft must fly. Stretch that same area into a longer, thinner wing and you have chosen its Reynolds number too. The planform is where every abstract choice cashes out as a physical object.

1Wing loading: weight spread over wing

The single most decisive number for how an aircraft flies is its wing loading — the weight it has to support divided by the wing area that supports it. Light wing, big area → low loading. Heavy load, small area → high loading.3

W = m · g W/S = wing loading Weight W in newtons (mass m in kg × g = 9.81 m/s²); area S in m². Aeronautics quotes loading in N/m²; the RC-model world prefers g/dm² (grams per square decimetre). They measure the same thing: 1 g/dm² = 0.981 N/m², so g/dm² ≈ (W/S) ⁄ 0.981. A 1.5 kg model on 0.30 m² carries ~49 N/m² ≈ 50 g/dm² — a typical light sport/soarer value.

Why does loading rule everything? Because the wing has to make exactly enough lift to hold the weight up. Spread the same weight over more wing and each square metre works less hard — which, as the next section shows, means the aircraft can fly slower to stay aloft.

2The speed your loading demands

In steady, level flight lift equals weight. Set the lift equation equal to W and solve for speed:

½ · ρ · V² · S · CL = W ⇒ V = √( 2W ⁄ (ρ · S · CL) ) ρ = 1.225 kg/m³ (sea level). Because W and ρ are fixed, V ∝ 1/√(S · CL) — equivalently V = √(2·(W/S)/(ρ·CL)). Lower wing loading, or a higher operating CL, means a slower flight speed.1

That proportionality is the whole point of this lesson. Slow flight is precisely what endurance wants: back in F1 you learned that staying aloft longest is a minimum-power problem, and power is drag × speed — so flying slowly is half the battle. Two levers buy that slowness: a low wing loading (big wing for the weight) or a high CL (fly near the top of the lift curve). Tennekes' famous "great flight diagram" shows the same law across every flyer from a fruit fly to a 747: the lighter your loading, the slower and gentler you fly.2

There is a floor under that speed, though — the stall speed. You cannot fly slower than the speed at which the wing, even at its maximum lift coefficient, can no longer hold the weight:

Vstall = √( 2W ⁄ (ρ · S · CLmax) ) , CLmax ≈ 1.4 Same equation, run at the wing's ceiling CLmax instead of your cruise CL. Your design must cruise comfortably above this. The margin is V/Vstall = √(CLmax/CL) — so a design CL close to 1.4 flies uncomfortably near the stall, exactly the thin margin F2 warned about for endurance.

3From aspect ratio to span and chord

Wing loading fixed your speed; aspect ratio now fixes your shape. AR is the ratio of span to chord — for a simple rectangular wing, AR = b²/S. Hold the area S constant and AR alone decides whether that area is a short stubby paddle or a long slender blade:

b = √( AR · S ) c = S ⁄ b = √( S ⁄ AR ) Re = V · c ⁄ ν Span b and chord c from area and aspect ratio (ν = 1.5×10⁻⁵ m²/s). Raising AR at fixed area lengthens the span and shrinks the chord — and chord is the length scale in the Reynolds number, so high AR drags Re downward.

Here is the trap the whole course has been circling. F3 taught you that high aspect ratio cuts induced drag — long, thin wings leak less energy off their tips. F4 taught you that low Reynolds number wrecks airfoil performance — thin laminar boundary layers separate and form drag-producing bubbles. But raising AR to win the first fight shrinks the chord, which lowers Reynolds number and loses the second. The two best pieces of advice in the course pull in opposite directions, and the planform is where you are forced to broker the deal. The sizer below lets you feel exactly where it bites.

5The wing-loading trade

There is no universally "best" wing loading — only the one that fits your mission. For an endurance drone the pull is toward the low end, but it is a genuine trade, paid in size and fragility:

Lowering wing loading buys slow, efficient flight — and charges you in metres of wingspan and grams of structure.
What you care aboutLow wing loadingHigh wing loading
Flight speedSlow — V ∝ √(W/S)Fast
Power to stay aloftLow — good for enduranceHigh — drains the battery
Wing sizeLarge span & areaCompact, hangar-friendly
StructureLight, floppy, flexes in gustsStiff, robust
Gust & wind handlingTossed around; hard in windPenetrates wind, steady
Reynolds (if AR is pushed)Risks tiny chord → low ReEasier to keep chord healthy
★ The planform verdict

Endurance wants low wing loading (to fly slow and sip power) and high aspect ratio (to cut induced drag). Both push toward a big, slender wing — and both quietly shrink the chord, dragging Reynolds number down toward the regime where airfoils misbehave. A good endurance wing is therefore the largest, slimmest wing whose chord still holds a healthy Reynolds number. That single sentence is the optimisation D1 will make you defend.

What you can now say

  1. Wing loading W/S (in N/m², or g/dm² with 1 g/dm² = 0.981 N/m²) is the single number that, with CL, fixes how fast an aircraft must fly.
  2. V = √(2W/(ρ·S·CL)), so V ∝ 1/√(S·CL) — lower loading or higher CL means slower, lower-power flight, which is what endurance wants.
  3. Vstall uses the same equation at CLmax ≈ 1.4; cruise must stay above it, and a high design CL leaves a thin margin.
  4. From area and aspect ratio, b = √(AR·S) and c = √(S/AR), and the chord sets Re = V·c/ν.
  5. High AR and low loading both fight for a big, slim wing — but both shrink the chord and drag Reynolds down. The endurance wing is the largest, slimmest one whose chord keeps Re healthy.
