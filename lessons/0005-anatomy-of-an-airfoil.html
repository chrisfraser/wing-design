Anatomy of an Airfoil

An airfoil is just a handful of geometric knobs with names. Learn the names — and what twisting each one does — and a cryptic code like “NACA 2412” turns into a recipe you can read.

⏱ ~11 minutes• 🧮 A little geometry — the shape made literal• Builds on F2 & F4
◎ Why this lesson

In F4 you learned that your 1–2 m drone flies deep in the low-Reynolds regime, where airfoil shape matters more than it does for a full-size aircraft. Next lesson (F6) you'll compare real airfoils and shortlist one for endurance — but you can't read an airfoil's data sheet until you can read its shape. This lesson hands you the vocabulary and the famous NACA 4-digit code, then shows what each shape knob actually does to lift, stall and drag.

Strip a wing down to a single slice and you get an airfoil — the 2-D cross-section that does the lifting. Every airfoil, from a paper dart to an airliner, is described by the same five or six measurements. Master them once and they never change.

1The parts of an airfoil

Lay the section flat with the nose pointing into the wind and there are only a few things to name:1

  • The leading edge (LE, the rounded nose) and the trailing edge (TE, the sharp tail) — where the air arrives and where it leaves.
  • The chord line: the straight line from LE to TE. Its length is the chord, c — the airfoil's “ruler.” Every other measurement is quoted as a percentage of c.
  • The mean camber line: the line running halfway between the upper and lower surfaces. How far it bows away from the chord line is the section's camber — its built-in curvature.
  • The thickness distribution: how fat the section is, measured perpendicular to the camber line, all the way along.

From those lines come the four numbers that define a 4-digit section: the maximum camber and where along the chord it peaks, and the maximum thickness and where it peaks. A symmetric airfoil is the special case where the camber line lies exactly on the chord line — zero camber.

2The NACA 4-digit code

In the 1930s, NACA (NASA's predecessor) ran families of airfoils through wind tunnels and gave each a number that is its geometry.2 The 4-digit code packs three of our measurements into four digits — M P XX:

NACA M P X X M = maximum camber, in % of chord (1 digit). P = position of that max camber, in tenths of chord (1 digit). XX = maximum thickness, in % of chord (2 digits). The max thickness always sits at 30% chord for this family.

So NACA 2412 reads: 2% camber, peaking at 4→40% of the chord, with a 12%-thick body. NACA 0012 has a leading 0 — no camber — so it's a symmetric 12% section. Same alphabet, two very different wings. 4What each knob actually does

The shape isn't decoration — every knob trades one flying quality for another. Here is what each digit buys you:

The four shape knobs of a NACA 4-digit section and what turning each one up does.
Shape knobIn the codeTurn it up and…
Max camber M1st digit the whole lift curve shifts up — more CL at every angle, and a more negative zero-lift angle.
Camber position P2nd digit the lift load moves fore or aft; further back can raise peak lift but adds nose-down pitching moment.
Max thickness XX3rd–4th digits the stall softens and there's room for a spar — but profile drag climbs, and worse so at low Reynolds.

Camber lifts the whole curve

Add camber and the air gets turned harder even when the section is pointed straight into the wind. The result: at any given angle of attack the section makes more lift than a symmetric one, and it keeps making lift down to a negative angle — its zero-lift angle goes more negative.1 In the language of F2, more camber shifts the entire CL–α line bodily upward. For a slow endurance wing that wants to fly at a high lift coefficient, that built-in lift is exactly what you're shopping for.

Thickness: gentler stall, but a drag bill

A thicker section has a blunter nose, so the airflow separates more gradually — the stall arrives softly rather than as a sudden drop, and the fat body gives your spar somewhere to live. The price is more drag. And here's the sting that ties back to F4: at the low Reynolds numbers your drone flies at, the boundary layer is thick and fragile, so that thickness penalty bites harder than the textbook high-Reynolds numbers suggest.1 Thin enough to keep drag down, thick enough to stall gently and hold a spar — that balance is the whole game, and it's why most low-Re model sections land around 8–12% thick.

★ The shape-to-behaviour map

Camber sets how much lift (shifts the curve up). Thickness sets how gently it stalls and how much drag you pay. Reading those two knobs off a NACA code tells you, before you ever open a wind-tunnel plot, roughly how a section will behave.

What you can now say

  1. An airfoil is named by a few lines: chord line, mean camber line, and a thickness distribution between leading and trailing edges.
  2. The NACA 4-digit code is M P XX: max camber %, camber position in tenths, max thickness % — e.g. 2412 = 2% camber at 40% chord, 12% thick.
  3. A leading 0 (like 0012) means symmetric — no camber.
  4. More camber shifts the lift curve up: more CL at a given angle and a more negative zero-lift angle.
  5. More thickness softens the stall and houses a spar, but costs drag — and that penalty grows at your low Reynolds numbers.
