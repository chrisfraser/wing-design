When to Reach for CFD

The last skill of a wing designer isn't running the fanciest solver — it's knowing which solver the question actually deserves, and where the cheap ones lie to you.

⏱ ~14 minutes• 🧠 Knowledge-heavy — judgement, not math• Final lesson · ties the course together
◎ Why this lesson

In D1 you synthesised a whole wing and read its endurance figure of merit off panel-method numbers. Sooner or later someone will ask the obvious question: “shouldn't you run real CFD?” The honest answer is sometimes — and reaching for a million-cell mesh when XFOIL would do is as much a mistake as trusting a naive CFD run that quietly over-predicts your drag. This lesson hands you the decision rule, and the one caution that bites every low-Reynolds beginner.

Every analysis tool is a bargain: more physics for more effort. The art is spending the least effort that still answers your question honestly. So before you mesh anything, climb the fidelity ladder and stop on the lowest rung that tells the truth.

1The fidelity ladder

The tools you've met sit on a ladder from cheap-and-approximate to costly-and-complete. Each rung buys more of the real flow physics — and charges you in setup time, compute, and the number of ways you can get it wrong.1

XFOIL ≪ XFLR5 ≪ RANS CFD 2-D viscous panel → 3-D panel + lifting-line/VLM with 2-D viscous strips → full 3-D viscous, meshed. Cost climbs by roughly an order of magnitude at every step; so does the effort to get a trustworthy answer.
  • XFOIL — a 2-D panel method coupled to a viscous boundary-layer model (Drela, MIT). It gives you an airfoil's CL, CD and the drag bucket at your Reynolds number in seconds. It knows nothing about the third dimension.
  • XFLR5 — wraps XFOIL's 2-D polars and stacks them across a finite wing using lifting-line or a vortex-lattice method (VLM). You get whole-wing lift distribution, induced drag, span efficiency and the wing's CL1.5/CD — minutes of work, still on a laptop.
  • RANS CFD — computational fluid dynamics that solves the Reynolds-averaged Navier–Stokes equations on a mesh of the actual 3-D geometry (OpenFOAM, SU2). It can capture separation, vortices, and bodies interacting — at the price of meshing, hours of compute, and a long list of modelling choices that each change the answer.2
The three rungs at a glance — match the tool to the question, not to your ambition.

2When panel methods are enough — and when they aren't

For the wing you designed in this course, the cheap rungs are usually enough. Reach no higher than XFOIL / XFLR5 when the flow is well-behaved:

✓ XFOIL / XFLR5 is enough when…

the flow is attached (no big separation), the geometry is a clean wing or airfoil whose flow is mildly 2-D, and you're doing preliminary design: sweeping airfoils, aspect ratios, taper, and reading off polars and CL1.5/CD at your Reynolds number. This covers the vast majority of the decisions in Phases 1–4.

Climb to RANS CFD only when the question contains physics the lower rungs structurally cannot see:

★ Escalate to CFD when…

the flow is genuinely three-dimensional or separated (post-stall behaviour, a laminar bubble bursting, blunt bodies); there is propeller–wing interaction or slipstream to capture; fuselage / wing-root junction effects matter; or you want shape optimisation driven by a high-fidelity gradient (SU2's adjoint solver). These are exactly the things a panel method has no equation for.

3The low-Reynolds trap

Here is the single most important caution in this lesson — the one that quietly ruins beginners' first CFD studies of model-scale wings.

⚠ Naive RANS over-predicts low-Re drag

Standard turbulence models (Spalart–Allmaras, k-ω SST, …) assume the boundary layer is fully turbulent everywhere. At our Reynolds numbers (~105) a large part of the airfoil actually runs laminar, with a laminar separation bubble near the leading edge. A fully-turbulent model misses both — it paints turbulent skin friction over the laminar runs and smears the bubble — so it over-predicts CD, sometimes badly, and mis-locates stall. The fix is a transition model (e.g. the γ–Reθ Langtry–Menter model) that lets the solver predict where laminar flow turns turbulent.3

CD(naive RANS, fully turbulent) > CD(real) at low Re Add a transition model — the laminar runs and the separation bubble reappear, and CD drops back toward measured values. This is precisely why XFOIL (which models transition by design) often beats a carelessly-run RANS case at low Reynolds number.

The irony is worth sitting with: an expensive solver, set up naively, can be less accurate than the cheap one. Fidelity is only potential accuracy — it has to be earned with the right physics switched on.

5A sane validation workflow

If you do climb to CFD, you only get to trust the absolute numbers after you've earned them. The order matters:

  1. Start cheap — XFOIL / XFLR5 first
    Get a baseline polar and a feel for the answer before any meshing. If the cheap rung already settles your question, stop here. Most of the time it does.
  2. Only escalate for a reason
    Move to RANS CFD when — and only when — your question contains 3-D / separated physics the panel methods can't see (§2). Write down what the cheap tools couldn't tell you.
  3. Prove mesh independence
    Refine the mesh until the forces stop changing. A result that shifts when you add cells isn't a result — it's a mesh artefact. Check y⁺ is in range for your turbulence model.
  4. Switch on a transition model at low Re
    Use γ–Reθ (Langtry–Menter) or equivalent so the laminar runs and the separation bubble are captured. Without it your low-Re drag is wrong by construction (§3).
  5. Validate against data before you believe a number
    Compare against measured UIUC polars or your XFOIL baseline at the same Re. Trust trends early; trust absolute drag only after it matches something real.

What you can now say

  1. Analysis tools form a fidelity ladder — XFOIL ≪ XFLR5 ≪ RANS CFD — and each rung trades more physics for more effort.
  2. XFOIL / XFLR5 is enough for attached, mildly-2-D, preliminary work: airfoil and whole-wing polars at your Reynolds number.
  3. Escalate to CFD only for genuinely 3-D / separated flow, propeller–wing interaction, junction effects, or shape optimisation.
  4. The low-Re trap: a fully-turbulent RANS model over-predicts drag — you must run a transition model (γ–Reθ) to capture laminar runs and bubbles.
  5. Trust CFD only after mesh independence and validation against measured data. Fidelity is potential accuracy; it must be earned.
