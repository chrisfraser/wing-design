Reading Real Airfoil Data

A published airfoil is really three plots in a trench coat. Learn to read them — the lift curve, the polar, and the endurance curve — and you can put three real low-Reynolds foils head to head and shortlist the one your drone wants.

⏱ ~13 minutes• 📊 Reading data — no new math• Builds on F4 & F5
◎ Why this lesson

In F5 you learned to read an airfoil's shape — camber, thickness, the NACA code. But shape only hints at behaviour. To actually choose a section you have to read its performance data: the curves that say how much lift, how much drag, and how gently it stalls — and crucially, at your Reynolds number. This lesson teaches you to read those plots, then pits three famous low-Re foils against each other and shortlists one for endurance. Next (T1) you'll generate this data yourself.

You don't pick an airfoil from a beauty contest of shapes — you pick it from its polars. Every serious airfoil comes with a small bundle of plots, and once you can read them, comparing two sections is as objective as comparing two lap times.

1An airfoil's data is three plots

Whether it comes from a wind tunnel or a solver, an airfoil's "data sheet" is the same three curves — each answering a different question:1

  • The lift curve, CL vs α: how lift grows with angle, the slope of that growth, and where it rolls over into stall. The top of this curve is CL,max; whether the top is a gentle dome or a sharp peak tells you how forgiving the stall is.
  • The drag polar, CL vs CD: lift plotted against the drag it costs. Low-drag sections show a drag bucket — a range of CL where CD stays near its minimum, the section's efficient operating band. You want your cruise lift to sit inside that bucket.
  • The endurance curve, CL1.5/CD vs CL: the figure of merit from F1, plotted across the foil's range. Its peak is the lift coefficient where this section flies longest.

The polar hides a beautiful trick. On it, L/D = CL/CD is just the slope of a line drawn from the origin to your operating point — so maximum L/D is the steepest such line, the one that grazes the curve as a tangent.1 That single geometric fact is how you read the best-range point straight off the plot.

2What endurance asks of the plots

Your mission is time aloft, so you read these plots with one priority order. For an endurance wing you want, in this order:

  • A high peak CL1.5/CD — the endurance figure of merit. This, not max L/D, is the headline (recall F1: range wants max L/D, endurance wants max CL1.5/CD, a higher-lift, slower point).
  • A low, wide drag bucket that spans your design lift coefficient — so the section stays efficient at the CL you actually cruise at, with margin for gusts.
  • A gentle stall — a rounded top to the lift curve, so an over-pull or a gust doesn't drop the wing.
  • All of the above read at your Reynolds number (~150–300k for a 1–2 m drone). A polar is not a property of the shape alone — the same foil is a different animal at a different Re, as F4 warned.
range → max CL / CD | endurance → max CL1.5 / CD Two different points on the same polar. On the CL–CD plot, max L/D is the origin tangent; the endurance peak sits above and to the right of it — higher lift, slower flight.

3The face-off — three real foils at Re 200,000

Here are three low-Re classics, with their real, published metrics at Re = 200,000.2 The Eppler E387 is the wind-tunnel reference foil; the Selig SD7037 is a popular thin model-glider section; the Selig S1223 is a famously high-lift, high-camber foil built for slow flight.3 Pick each and watch its polar — and the origin tangent that reads off its max L/D.

4The verdict — and the split nobody warns you about

Line the real numbers up and a clean story you expected — "one foil is best" — falls apart:

Notice the split. The E387 wins max L/D (84.4) — so it is the range champion, the foil that glides farthest per joule. But the S1223 wins the endurance figure of merit (98.5), because its enormous 8.1% camber makes huge lift, and endurance rewards high lift flown slowly. The catch is written right beside that win: the S1223 pays for it with nearly double the minimum drag (0.0176 vs 0.0092) and an endurance point way up at CL ≈ 2.03 — flying that slow, near the top of the lift curve, leaves little margin and gets more sensitive as Reynolds drops. The SD7037 is the modest all-rounder: the lowest Cd,min, but neither the L/D nor the endurance leader.

★ There is no single "best" airfoil

"Best" depends on the mission. For range, the E387's max L/D wins. For endurance — our mission — the S1223's high-lift CL1.5/CD wins, at the price of higher drag, a very high design CL, and slower, more delicate flight. The SD7037 splits the difference. Pick the metric that matches the job, then read the polar at the Reynolds number you'll actually fly.

The low-Re collapse — why "at your Reynolds" is in bold

Drop the Reynolds number to 100,000 and every one of these foils gets dramatically worse — the laminar separation bubble grows, thickening the boundary layer and piling on drag. Both the L/D and the endurance figure of merit collapse by roughly a third:2

The low-Re collapse: (L/D)max and (CL1.5/CD)max, Re 200k → 100k.
Airfoil(L/D)max · 200k → 100k(CL1.5/CD)max · 200k → 100k
Eppler E38784.4 → 60.789.0 → 65.6
Selig SD703774.8 → 55.272.7 → 54.8
Selig S122373.6 → 54.598.5 → 68.0

This is the core low-Reynolds lesson: a foil's polar is not a fixed property of its shape — it is a property of the shape and the Reynolds number. A section that looks brilliant at Re 500k can be mediocre at Re 100k. Always compare foils at the Re your wing will fly, and remember that shrinking the chord (to chase aspect ratio, F4) pushes you down into this collapse.

5These are XFOIL numbers — sanity-check them

Every number above is computed, not measured — XFOIL (or a similar viscous panel code) at Ncrit = 9. That's fine for comparing foils on equal terms, but XFOIL runs optimistic: it tends to over-predict L/D and CL,max versus a real wind tunnel, especially at low Re where the separation bubble is hard to model.

⚠ Computed ≠ measured — always validate

The E387 has been wind-tunnel tested for decades. Measured values come in below the solver: real (L/D)max ≈ 60–65 and CL,max ≈ 1.15–1.20 at Re 200k4 — noticeably less than the XFOIL 84.4 and 1.32 in our table. Treat computed polars as a ranking tool, not gospel numbers, and sanity-check against measured UIUC wind-tunnel data before you trust an absolute figure.

What you can now say

  1. An airfoil's data is three plots: the lift curve (CL–α, with stall), the drag polar (CL–CD, with its low-drag bucket), and the endurance curve (CL1.5/CD).
  2. On the polar, max L/D is the steepest line from the origin — the tangent; the endurance peak sits above and right of it, at higher lift and slower speed.
  3. For endurance you want a high CL1.5/CD peak, a drag bucket spanning your design CL, and a gentle stall — read at your Reynolds number.
  4. There's no single best foil: the E387 wins range (L/D 84.4), the S1223 wins endurance (FoM 98.5, at CL≈2.0), the SD7037 splits the difference. The mission picks the metric.
  5. These are computed (XFOIL) numbers — optimistic; drop Re from 200k to 100k and every foil loses ~30%. Always sanity-check against measured wind-tunnel data.
