Hands-on XFOIL

You've trusted other people's polars long enough. This lesson hands you the tool that made them — so you can generate your own, for your airfoil, at your Reynolds number.

⏱ ~14 minutes• 🧰 Hands-on — install & run a real tool• Builds on F6 · Phase 3 begins
◎ Why this lesson

In F6 you learned to read three plots and shortlist an airfoil for endurance. But every one of those curves came from someone else's run, at someone else's Reynolds number — and your wing won't fly at theirs. The fix is to stop borrowing polars and start producing them. XFOIL is the tool that made most of the low-Reynolds data you've been reading, and it runs in seconds on your laptop. By the end of this lesson you'll drive it yourself.

A published polar is a photograph of one wing in one tunnel on one day. To design your wing you need to ask your own questions — this airfoil, this Reynolds number, this angle sweep — and get an answer in seconds. That is exactly what XFOIL is for.

1What XFOIL is — and why it fits low Re

XFOIL is a free 2-D airfoil analysis code written by Mark Drela at MIT.1 It pairs a panel method — which models the airfoil as a ring of vortex/source panels to get the inviscid flow fast — with an integral boundary-layer solver that tracks where the flow goes laminar, where it transitions to turbulent, and where it separates. Coupling those two and iterating to a converged state is what lets it predict drag, not just lift.

That coupling is exactly why XFOIL is the standard tool for our regime. Down at Reynolds numbers of 100k–300k, performance is decided by the boundary layer — laminar separation, transition, the dreaded separation bubble — and a purely inviscid solver is blind to all of it. XFOIL models that physics directly, runs in seconds, and has been validated against decades of wind-tunnel data. For a 1–2 m model wing, it is the right first tool, every time.

◆ Two solvers, one answer

The panel method alone gives you an inviscid result: lift, pressure distribution, and zero profile drag. The boundary-layer model is what adds the friction and pressure drag that make a polar useful. XFOIL's trick is coupling them so each updates the other until both agree — a viscous polar. Keep that split in mind; the very next command you'll learn is the switch that turns the viscous half on.

2Get it installed

XFOIL is tiny and self-contained. Grab it from the official MIT page, which carries the binaries, the source, and the documentation:2

  • Windows — download the precompiled xfoil.exe (plus its support files) from the MIT page and run it from a terminal in that folder.
  • macOS / Linux — build from the published Fortran source on the same page, or install a community-packaged build (Homebrew on macOS, your distro's package manager on Linux often carry it).

You'll also need an airfoil's coordinate file — a plain-text .dat of x/y points. Download these from the UIUC airfoil coordinate database (e.g. e387.dat, sd7037.dat, s1223.dat) and keep them in the folder you launch XFOIL from. When it starts you'll see a bare XFOIL c> prompt — a tiny command language, not a GUI. The next section is your phrasebook.

3The workflow, command by command

A polar run is always the same short ritual: load a shape, clean up the panels, enter the operating menu, switch on viscosity, set the conditions, start a results file, and sweep the angle of attack.34 Type these at the prompt, one per line:

  • LOAD e387.dat
    Read the airfoil coordinates from disk. XFOIL now holds the E387's geometry.
  • PANE
    Re-panel the airfoil onto a clean, smoothly-spaced set of nodes. Better paneling means better accuracy and far fewer convergence headaches later.
  • OPER
    Enter the operating menu — this is where you set conditions and run analyses. The prompt changes to .OPERi c>.
  • VISC 200000
    Switch on the viscous boundary-layer solver and set the Reynolds number in one stroke (here 200,000). This is the command that turns an inviscid sketch into a real drag polar — without it you get no usable CD.
  • ITER 200
    Raise the iteration limit. Low-Re points near separation can be stubborn; 200 gives the viscous–inviscid coupling room to converge before XFOIL gives up.
  • MACH 0
    Set the freestream Mach number to zero — incompressible flow, which is correct for slow-flying models and keeps the solution clean.
  • VPAR → N 9 → (blank line)
    Open the viscous-parameters submenu, set Ncrit to 9 (the transition criterion — see below), then press Enter on an empty line to step back out to OPER.
  • PACC → e387_Re200k.txt → (blank line)
    Toggle polar accumulation on. XFOIL asks for a save filename — give it one — then asks for a separate dump file, which you decline with a blank line. Every point you run from now on is appended to that file.
  • ASEQ -4 14 0.5
    Run an alpha sequence: sweep the angle of attack from −4° to 14° in 0.5° steps. XFOIL solves each angle and writes CL, CD, and CM into the polar file — that's your finished curve.
  • (blank line) → QUIT
    Press Enter to leave OPER, then QUIT to exit. Your e387_Re200k.txt now holds the polar — plot it in XFOIL, a spreadsheet, or whatever you like.

Typed end to end, the whole session reads like this — the two blank lines are real and load-bearing (they're the Enter presses that back out of VPAR and decline the dump file):

LOAD e387.dat
PANE
OPER
VISC 200000
ITER 200
MACH 0
VPAR
N 9

PACC
e387_Re200k.txt

ASEQ -4 14 0.5
◆ What Ncrit means — and why VISC is non-negotiable

Ncrit is the exponent in XFOIL's eN transition model. A tiny disturbance in the laminar boundary layer grows exponentially; XFOIL trips the flow to turbulent once that growth reaches a factor of eNcrit. So Ncrit is really a knob for how calm the air is: higher means quieter air and later transition.

Sensible values: Ncrit ≈ 9 for a clean, low-turbulence wind tunnel or very smooth air; ≈ 7 for average free-flight; ~5 or lower for turbulent, gusty, or rough/dirty conditions. Pick the one that matches where your drone actually flies — it shifts the drag polar noticeably.

And the non-negotiable part: VISC <Re> is mandatory for a drag polar. Skip it and XFOIL stays inviscid — it will happily report lift and CM but zero profile drag, which is useless for endurance work. Viscous on, Reynolds set: always.

5Trust, but verify: validate against measured data

XFOIL is fast, free, and remarkably good — which makes it easy to forget it is still a model. Before you bet a wing on a polar, check it against real wind-tunnel measurements at your Reynolds number. The UIUC Summary of Low-Speed Airfoil Data is the gold standard for exactly the foils and Reynolds numbers we care about.5

⚠ XFOIL can be optimistic about drag at very low Re

Inside a laminar separation bubble — common below Re ≈ 200k — XFOIL's boundary-layer model tends to under-predict the drag the bubble actually costs, so its CD can read lower than the tunnel measures and its peak L/D higher. Lift is usually closer to the truth than drag. The habit that keeps you honest: trust XFOIL for trends (which airfoil is better, how a change moves the polar) but verify the magnitudes against measured drag polars before committing. Run XFOIL at the same Re and Ncrit as the published data and compare the curves directly.

What you can now say

  1. XFOIL couples a panel method (fast inviscid flow) with a boundary-layer solver — that pairing is what lets it predict drag at low Re.
  2. The polar ritual: LOAD → PANE → OPER → VISC Re → ITER → MACH 0 → VPAR/N → PACC/file → ASEQ.
  3. VISC <Re> is mandatory: no VISC means an inviscid run with zero profile drag.
  4. Ncrit is the eN transition exponent — ≈9 clean, ≈7 average free-flight, ~5 turbulent — and it shifts the whole polar.
  5. XFOIL can be optimistic on drag inside low-Re separation bubbles, so always validate magnitudes against measured UIUC data.
