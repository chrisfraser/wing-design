Build the Wing

The last lesson. Every number you've computed now becomes a real object — a foam-core composite panel you can hold, balance, and fly.

⏱ ~12 minutes• 🔧 Hands-on — the build bench• Final lesson · builds on B1 & B2
◎ Why this lesson

In B2 you chose a construction method and set a weight budget; in B1 you sized the spar that carries the load. Now you actually make it. This lesson walks the consensus build sequence for a vacuum-bagged foam-core composite wing — the method most long-endurance modellers converge on because it's tough, repeatable, and light. By the end you'll have a step-by-step plan, a tick-off shopping list, and the discipline to keep the finished wing as light as you designed it.

A wing is the one part of the aircraft you cannot fudge: it carries every gram into the air and it is the first thing to fail if you get the loads — or the weight — wrong. The good news is that the build is a recipe. Follow it in order, respect the two places it punishes carelessness, and a first-time bagged wing comes out straight, stiff, and on budget.

1The build sequence

This is the order experienced builders settle on for a foam-core composite panel: cut the shape, put the strength in, skin it, squeeze it under vacuum, join the halves, finish, and finally weigh and balance against your plan.1 Do them out of order and you fight yourself — you can't slot a spar into a skinned core, and you can't bag a wing you've already joined.

  1. Hot-wire cut the cores
    Bow a length of nichrome wire taut on a wooden frame and clamp a root and a tip airfoil template to your EPS or XPS foam block. A steady current melts the bow cleanly through both templates at once, leaving an accurate, washed-out core. Keep the leftover foam “shucks” — those negative offcuts become the cradle that supports the skin evenly under vacuum. Finish with a light sand.
  2. Cut the spar slot & install the spar
    Groove a straight channel with a Dremel cut-off disc, using strips of balsa as depth-stops so you can't plunge too deep. Bed the carbon spar — caps top and bottom, sized the way B1 showed — in epoxy thickened with microballoons. (Alternatively, lay unidirectional carbon caps directly inside the skin during layup and skip the slot.)
  3. Skin / glass the cores
    Wet out roughly 0.7 oz (≈24 g/m²) glass cloth on waxed Mylar sheets, often laid on the bias (45°) so the weave resists twist, i.e. torsion. Add a doubler at the root, where the load is highest, and an extra layer over the leading edge for hangar-rash and impact.2
  4. Vacuum bag
    Fold the Mylar-and-glass skin over the core, then stack peel-ply + perforated release film + breather and seal it all in the bag. Pull a gentle vacuum — about 6–8 inHg for soft white EPS, 12–16 inHg for tougher blue XPS — supporting the part on its shucks so it can't crush or warp. Leave it to cure 24 hours or more.3
  5. Joiner & dihedral
    Fit a ply or carbon joiner box at each root and epoxy it in. Bring the two panels together on a flat reference table to set the dihedral angle and lock the wing true — a twist set now is a twist forever.
  6. Finish
    Fill any pinholes with lightweight filler, block-sand through progressively finer grit (80 → 400), and either leave the bare glass finish or cover and paint. Resist the urge to pile on filler and paint — it's pure weight.
  7. Balance & weigh
    Weigh each panel and confirm the two sides match. Balance the assembled wing to the design centre of gravity — roughly 25–33% of the chord — and put the whole thing on the scale. Then compare that number to the structural mass you budgeted in B2. This is the moment of truth.
Prefer balsa and film? The built-up alternative

You don't have to bag composites. The traditional path is a built-up structure: cut a stack of ribs to the airfoil shape, thread them onto the spars, sheet the leading-edge “D-box” for torsional stiffness, then shrink heat-shrink film (Oracover, Monokote) over the frame with an iron. It's often the lightest option for a given size and trivially repairable, but it's more fragile and less stiff in torsion than a bagged composite skin, and an open structure needs careful jigging to come out straight.5 The build order is the same idea — shape, then strength, then skin, then join, then balance.

⚠ The weight thieves

Four mistakes quietly steal the endurance you designed for. Watch every one:

  • Excess epoxy / resin — the #1 avoidable mass. Cured resin beyond what wets the cloth adds weight and no strength. Squeeze it out under vacuum.
  • Warped cores — a twisted panel flies with built-in error you can never trim out. Cut on a flat block; bag on the shucks; join on a flat table.
  • A weak root / spar joint — the root is the highest-load region in the whole wing (B1). Skimp here and the wing folds here.
  • A nose-heavy CG — too much mass forward of ~25–33% chord forces extra trim drag and dulls the handling. Balance it; don't hope.

2Closing the loop: weigh against your budget

The build only counts as a success if it honours the plan. Two cheap checks — a scale and a balance point — close the loop back to everything earlier in the course:

CG @ 25–33 % chord · mbuilt ≤ mbudget → endurance preserved Balance the wing to a CG at roughly a quarter-to-a-third of the chord, and weigh the finished panels against the structural mass you set in B2. Over budget means higher wing loading, a faster flight speed, and an operating point that has drifted off your CL1.5/CD peak — the endurance you designed has quietly leaked away.

The whole journey — F1 to B3

Step back and look at the arc you just walked, from “what does optimal even mean?” to a finished wing on the bench:

  1. F1–F4 · Foundations. You defined “better” as a single number — max CL1.5/CD for endurance, not max L/D — learned how a wing really turns flow into lift, split drag into parasitic and induced, and met the low-Reynolds regime your small drone actually lives in.
  2. F5–T1 · The airfoil. You read camber and thickness off a section, compared real low-Re foils on their polars, and ran a viscous polar in XFOIL at your Reynolds number.
  3. T2–T3 · The planform. You turned the aspect-ratio insight into a real chord, span, area and wing loading, then checked the whole 3-D wing's span efficiency and CL1.5/CD in XFLR5.
  4. D1–D2 · The design. You synthesised it all into one endurance wing on a live dashboard, and learned when CFD is worth reaching for — and when it would only mislead you.
  5. B1–B3 · The build. You sized the spar as a cantilever beam, chose materials against a weight budget, and — in this lesson — actually built the wing and weighed it back against that budget.

You understood lift and drag, picked a foil at your Reynolds number, sized a planform, designed the wing, and built it. There's only one thing left to do — go fly it.
