Materials & Construction: the Weight Budget

B1 sized the spar that keeps the wing from folding. Now we have to build the whole thing — and every gram we add is paid back, with interest, in lost endurance.

⏱ ~13 minutes• 🧮 Light arithmetic — a weight budget• Builds on B1 · The Wing as a Beam
◎ Why this lesson

Back in T2 we proved that a low wing loading is what lets an endurance drone fly slow and sip power. But wing loading is just weight ÷ area — so the build itself sets it. Pick a heavy construction method, or drown the wing in epoxy, and you raise the loading and shorten the flight, no matter how clean the airfoil. This lesson gives you a weight budget: a way to choose a construction method, predict what the wing will weigh, and check the loading before you cut foam. It turns B1's spar into a finished, flyable wing.

There is no such thing as a free gram. Lift it, carry it, and your battery pays for it the whole flight. So the structural question is never just “will it hold?” — it's “will it hold while weighing almost nothing?” That single constraint decides which materials and which build you should reach for.

1The number that picks a material: stiffness per gram

Ask which material is stiffest and the answer is steel or aluminium. Ask which is stiffest per kilogram and the ranking flips entirely. What a flying structure cares about is specific stiffness (E/ρ) and specific strength (σ/ρ) — the modulus and the working stress divided by density. A spar cap can always be made stiffer by adding material; the only thing it can't escape is the weight that comes with it.2

Per kilogram, carbon is in a class of its own — and wood quietly matches aluminium. Representative values; real laminates vary with fibre fraction, grain and resin content.

Read the E/ρ column. Aluminium, spruce and balsa are all clustered near 26 MJ/kg — for a given stiffness, a wooden spar weighs about the same as an aluminium one. Carbon sits at 92, roughly 3.5× the specific stiffness of any of them, and its specific strength leaves metal and wood far behind.5 That is the whole logic of a modern model wing: concentrate carbon where the bending lives — the spar caps, the high-stress region B1 identified — and use cheap, ultralight balsa or foam everywhere the loads are low, the cores and the ribs that only have to hold the airfoil shape.

2Three honest ways to build a wing

Those materials get assembled in one of three classic ways. Each has a characteristic structural areal density — grams of wing structure per square decimetre of area — and each buys its lightness or toughness at a different price. The figures below are representative (REP); your fibre, foam and especially your resin discipline move them up or down.3

Representative (REP) structural areal densities and the trade each method makes for an endurance wing.
Method Rep. areal density Strengths Watch-outs for endurance
Hot-wire foam core + glass/carbon skin ≈ 11 g/dm² Accurate, stiff airfoil straight off the templates; tough and ding-resistant. Resin discipline is everything — excess epoxy is pure dead weight.
Built-up balsa ribs + heat-shrink film ≈ 6 g/dm² The lightest open framework; cheap, and easy to repair after a hard landing. Film sags and can flutter; weak in torsion as aspect ratio climbs.
Moulded composite (carbon/glass over foam) ≈ 9 g/dm² Best airfoil accuracy and torsional stiffness for thin, high-AR wings; durable. Needs tooling (moulds); hard to repair in the field.

Notice the moulded wing can come out lighter than the foam-core one (≈ 9 vs ≈ 11 g/dm²): light spread-tow skins put fibre exactly where it's needed and nowhere else, which is precisely what a thin, high-aspect-ratio endurance wing wants. The built-up frame is lighter still, but its film skin and weaker torsion box make it the gamble at high AR. There is no universal winner — only the lightest method you can build well.

3How light is light enough?

To know whether your number is good, you need targets. For our mission the pull is firmly toward the light end — the F5J / floater band. A lighter wing flies slower, and slower flight is exactly the minimum-power, maximum-CL1.5/CD condition the whole course steers by. Construction isn't a footnote to the aerodynamics; it is the aerodynamics, expressed in grams.

4The weight budget

Put it together and you get a budget you can compute before touching a tool. The construction method fixes the areal density; multiply by area for the wing's structural mass; add the payload it has to carry; divide by area for the loading:

mwing = areal density · S AUW = mwing + mpayload W/S = AUW / S Areal density in g/dm² (set by the construction method), wing area S in dm². Every gram of structure adds straight onto all-up weight AUW and pushes the wing loading W/S up — which forces a faster flight speed and a bigger power bill. 1 g/dm² = 0.981 N/m².

What you can now say

  1. Structural materials are picked by specific stiffness (E/ρ) and specific strength (σ/ρ), not raw E or σ — which is why carbon dominates the caps and wood merely ties aluminium.
  2. Put the expensive carbon where the bending lives (the spar caps, from B1) and use ultralight balsa or foam for the low-stress cores and ribs.
  3. Three build routes, by representative areal density: built-up balsa ≈ 6 g/dm² (lightest, most fragile), moulded composite ≈ 9 (most accurate), foam-core skin ≈ 11 (toughest).
  4. The weight budget: wing mass = areal density × area; add payload for AUW; AUW ÷ area = wing loading.
  5. Every gram raises the loading, which forces faster flight and more power — so building light is the endurance goal, the same north star (max CL1.5/CD) the course has chased since F1.
