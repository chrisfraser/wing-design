The Two Kinds of Drag

Why three-quarters of your endurance drag comes from one source — and what to do about it.

Why this, now
In Lesson 1 drag was a single curve. But “reduce drag” is useless advice until you know which drag. Split it in two and a clear, surprising design lever falls out — the one that matters most for staying aloft.

Total drag on your wing is two different bills added together. One you pay just for moving through air. The other you pay specifically for making lift — and on an endurance wing, it's by far the larger of the two.

Bill #1 — Parasitic drag

The cost of pushing a shape through air, whether or not it's lifting. It's mostly skin friction (air dragging along the wing's surface in the boundary layer) plus a little pressure drag (the wake behind the shape). In coefficient form we call this floor CD0, and to a first approximation it doesn't change with lift.It's not perfectly constant — a good airfoil has a low-drag “bucket” over a range of angles, and profile drag creeps up near stall. That fine structure is set by the airfoil and its Reynolds number — the next two lessons.

Bill #2 — Induced drag

This one is the price of lift itself, and it only exists because your wing has ends. High pressure under the wing and low pressure over it means air leaks around the tips, rolling into two trailing vortices. Those vortices push a downward flow (downwash) across the whole wing, tilting the lift vector slightly backward. That backward tilt is a drag force you created purely by lifting:

CDi = CL2 ⁄ (π · AR · e)

AR = aspect ratio (how long and slender the wing is, span²/area). e = span efficiency (≈0.85 for a typical wing; 1.0 is the ideal elliptical loading). Two facts hide in this little equation, and they govern your whole design: induced drag grows with lift squared, and it shrinks as aspect ratio rises. [NASA Glenn]

The reveal

Add the bills: CD = CD0 + CL2/(π·AR·e) — that's the parabolic drag polar from Lesson 1, now decoded. Parasitic drag rules at low lift (fast flight); induced drag rules at high lift (slow flight). They're exactly equal at max L/D. And at your max-endurance point, induced drag is three times the parasitic — so ~75% of your endurance drag is induced. Beat induced drag and you win endurance.

Your master lever for endurance

Because endurance flight is induced-drag-dominated, the highest-leverage move is a long, slender, high-aspect-ratio wing — that's why sailplanes and long-endurance UAVs look the way they do. But it isn't free: more span means more structural weight and bending, and a narrower chord means a lower Reynolds number, which can hurt the airfoil. Holding those trade-offs in tension is the real design game — and Reynolds number is next.

From memory.

At the best-endurance operating point, most of the wing's drag is…

At max CL1.5/CD, induced drag is three times the parasitic — about 75% of the total. That's why endurance design is mostly a fight against induced drag.

The most effective way to cut induced drag for endurance is to…

Induced drag = CL²/(π·AR·e), so a higher aspect ratio (longer, slenderer wing) directly lowers it. A thinner airfoil mainly affects parasitic drag; flying faster lowers the required CL but isn't what endurance wants.

As the lift coefficient increases, induced drag grows…

CDi ∝ CL². Doubling lift quadruples induced drag — which is exactly why flying slowly (high CL) makes induced drag the dominant cost.
