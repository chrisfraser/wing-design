The Two Kinds of Drag

Why three-quarters of your endurance drag comes from a single source — and the one design lever that beats it.

⏱ ~12 minutes• 🧮 Light math — one equation that matters• Builds on F1
◎ Why this lesson

In F1, drag was a single curve and “reduce drag” was the whole story. But that advice is useless until you know which drag. Split total drag into its two sources and a clear — and surprising — design lever falls out: the single biggest move you can make for staying aloft. By the end of this lesson you'll know what it is and why it works.

Total drag on your wing is two different bills added together. One you pay just for moving a shape through air. The other you pay specifically for making lift — and on an endurance wing, it is by far the larger of the two.

1Bill #1 — parasitic drag

The first bill is the cost of pushing a shape through air, whether or not it is lifting. It is mostly skin friction — air dragging along the wing's surface inside the boundary layer — plus a little pressure drag from the wake left behind the shape. In coefficient form we call this floor CD0, and to a first approximation it does not change with lift.3

It is not perfectly constant — a good airfoil has a low-drag “bucket” over a range of angles, and profile drag creeps up near stall. But that fine structure is set by the airfoil and its Reynolds number, which are the next two lessons. For now, treat parasitic drag as a flat floor: CD0 = 0.018 for a representative low-Reynolds wing.

2Bill #2 — induced drag

The second bill is the price of lift itself, and it exists only because your wing has ends. High pressure under the wing and low pressure over it means air leaks around the tips, rolling into two trailing vortices. Those vortices wash a downward flow — downwash — across the whole wing, tilting the lift vector slightly backward. That backward tilt is a drag force you created purely by lifting:1

CDi = CL² / ( π · AR · e ) AR = aspect ratio — how long and slender the wing is (span²/area). e = span efficiency (≈ 0.85 for a typical wing; 1.0 is the ideal elliptical loading). Two facts hide in this little equation, and they govern your whole design: induced drag grows with lift squared, and it shrinks as aspect ratio rises.

3The reveal: add the bills

Stack the two bills on top of each other and you recover the parabolic drag polar from F1 — now decoded into its two ingredients:

CD = CD0 + CL² / ( π · AR · e ) The flat parasitic floor plus the induced term that climbs with CL2. Often written CD = CD0 + k·CL², with k = 1/(π·e·AR).

Parasitic drag rules at low lift (fast flight); induced drag rules at high lift (slow flight). They are exactly equal at max L/D. And at your max-endurance point — high lift, slow flight — induced drag is roughly three times the parasitic.2

★ The reveal

At the endurance operating point (around CL = 1.10), induced drag is about three times the parasitic drag — so roughly 75% of your endurance drag is induced. That single fact reframes the whole design problem: beat induced drag and you win endurance. Everything below follows from it.

5The master lever for endurance

Because endurance flight is induced-drag-dominated, the highest-leverage move you can make is the one that attacks the induced term directly. Look at the equation: induced drag falls as aspect ratio rises. Raise AR and three-quarters of your drag bill shrinks — without changing the airfoil at all.4

★ Your master lever

A long, slender, high-aspect-ratio wing is the single most effective endurance design choice — which is exactly why sailplanes and long-endurance UAVs all look the way they do. Raising AR from 8 to 16 cuts total endurance drag by nearly 40% at the same lift.

⚠ But it isn't free

More span means more structural weight and bending loads to carry. And holding wing area fixed, a higher aspect ratio means a narrower chord — which lowers the Reynolds number the airfoil sees, and a low Reynolds number can hurt the airfoil badly. Holding those trade-offs in tension is the real design game — and Reynolds number is next.

What you can now say

  1. Total drag splits into two bills: parasitic (CD0, a lift-independent floor of skin friction + pressure drag) and induced (the price of making lift).
  2. Induced drag is CDi = CL²/(π·AR·e) — it grows with lift squared and shrinks as aspect ratio rises.
  3. The two add to the parabolic polar CD = CD0 + CL²/(π·AR·e): parasitic rules at low lift, induced at high lift, equal at max L/D.
  4. At the endurance point, induced drag is ~3× parasitic — about 75% of the total — so endurance is a fight against induced drag.
  5. Raising aspect ratio is the master lever, but it costs structural weight and lowers the airfoil's Reynolds number — the next lesson.
