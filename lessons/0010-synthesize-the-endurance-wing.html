Synthesize the Endurance Wing

This is the capstone. Everything the course taught — airfoil, aspect ratio, area, Reynolds, span efficiency — collapses into one wing you can defend with a single number.

⏱ ~18 minutes• 🧮 Synthesis — bring every earlier lesson• Builds on F1 – T3
◎ Why this lesson

In T3 you took a 2-D airfoil polar all the way to a whole-wing CL1.5/CD, complete with a span-efficiency penalty. Every lesson before it handed you one more knob: the figure of merit (F1), real airfoil data (F6), aspect ratio (F3), wing loading (T2), Reynolds number (F4) and span efficiency (T3). Today you turn the knobs together and watch them fight. The output is not another fact — it is a finished wing, sized in metres, judged on its endurance number, and defended against the constraints that try to kill it.

A wing design is never a single right answer; it is the best deal you can broker between forces that pull in opposite directions. High aspect ratio cuts induced drag but starves Reynolds. A high lift coefficient floats slowly but creeps toward the stall. The capstone skill is reading all of those at once and naming the one constraint that is actually binding — then trading against it, not against whatever happens to be on your mind.

1The design loop, one last time

You already own every step. The only new thing is doing them in a circle until the numbers stop improving — and knowing which way to turn when one of them goes red.

  1. Pick an airfoil (F6)
    Choose a low-Reynolds section and read its two numbers: minimum drag Cd0 and maximum lift CLmax. A range champ like the E387 is slippery; a high-lift foil like the S1223 lifts hard but drags.
  2. Pick an aspect ratio (F3)
    Higher AR slashes induced drag and lifts the figure of merit — but at a fixed area it shrinks the chord, and the chord is what sets Reynolds.
  3. Pick area & wing loading (T2)
    Wing loading W/S fixes cruise speed; area and AR then fix span and chord. Low loading flies slow and sips power — the heart of endurance.
  4. Check Reynolds (F4)
    Is the chord long enough for clean low-Re flow? Below ~100k a laminar separation bubble wrecks the airfoil you so carefully chose.
  5. Check stall margin (F1 · T2)
    Cruise must sit comfortably above Vstall. A design CL pushed too close to CLmax leaves no room for a gust.
  6. Read the figure of merit — then iterate
    Compute the whole-wing CL1.5/CD. If a light is red, trade against that constraint and loop back to step 1.

2The number that judges it all

From F1: a propeller (or electric) aircraft loiters longest at minimum power, which lands at maximum CL1.5/CD — slower and at a higher lift coefficient than the max-L/D point that wins range.1 This is the whole-wing version, so the drag is the whole-wing drag from T3:

maximise CL1.5 / CD CD = Cd0 + CL² / (π · AR · e) Cd0 is the airfoil's profile drag (from F6); the second term is induced drag (F3), softened by span efficiency e (T3). Raising AR shrinks the induced term, so at a fixed CL the figure of merit climbs with AR — which is exactly why endurance wings grow long and slender until something stops them.
ρ = 1.225 kg/m³ ν = 1.5×10⁻⁵ m²/s g = 9.81 m/s² e = 0.85 W = m·g b = √(AR·S) c = √(S/AR) V = √(2W / (ρ·S·CL)) Vstall = √(2W / (ρ·S·CLmax)) Re = V·c / ν These are the same equations from T2 and T3, now run all at once on one design point. The span efficiency e = 0.85 is a deliberately conservative whole-wing value: a well-tapered wing reaches e ≈ 0.95–0.99 (T3), so treat 0.85 as a safety margin, not a target.2

4Reading the verdict

The figure of merit alone is a trap: the gold ridge sits at the very top of the map, deep inside the Reynolds and span forbidden zones. A wing that scores 30 but cannot fly is worth nothing. So the dashboard never lets you read the number without also reading the chips — and when a chip turns red, there is exactly one right way to push back on it.

What each red light means, and the lever that actually moves it. The wrong lever wastes a design cycle.
Red lightWhat went wrongThe fix that works
Reynolds < 100kThe chord is too short for clean low-Re flow — a bubble is forming.Lower the AR (a wider chord), ease the design CL, or add mass. Not a thinner airfoil — thickness doesn't change Reynolds.
Span > 2.2 mToo big to build, transport, or launch from a bench.Raise the wing loading (smaller area) or lower the AR.
Stall margin < 1.2×Cruise is dangerously close to the stall; a gust could drop you.Lower the design CL, or pick a higher-CLmax airfoil (the S1223).

⚠ The Reynolds trap that fools everyone

"Just make the wing bigger" feels like it should raise Reynolds — more wing, more chord. It doesn't. Plug the level-flight speed into Re = V·c/ν and the area cancels clean away:

Re = √( 2·m·g / (ρ · CL · AR) ) / ν

At a fixed mass and design CL, Reynolds depends only on aspect ratio. Grow the area and the wing loading and span drop, but cruise slows in exact proportion — the chord gets longer and the air gets slower, and they cancel. That is why, on the map, the Reynolds zone is a flat horizontal ceiling: the only in-flight lever for it is AR (or changing mass / CL). The "bigger chord" you actually need comes from lowering AR.

5A worked example, defended

Tempting first cut: a light 1.5 kg airframe, the friendly SD7037 section, a slender AR = 11, an area of 0.33 m² (so W/S ≈ 45 N/m²), cruising at a healthy endurance CL = 1.0. Press Load worked example to drop the dot exactly here, then follow the arithmetic.

W = m·g = 1.5 × 9.81 = 14.7 N · W/S = 44.6 N/m² · S = 0.33 m²
b = √(11 × 0.33) = 1.91 m · c = √(0.33 / 11) = 0.173 m
V = √(2·14.7 / (1.225·0.33·1.0)) = 8.5 m/s · Vstall = 7.4 m/s · margin = 1.15×
Re = 8.5 × 0.173 / 1.5e-5 = 98,500 (just under 100k)
CD = 0.0090 + 1.0² / (π·11·0.85) = 0.0090 + 0.0341 = 0.043
FoM = CL1.5 / CD = 1.0 / 0.043 = 23.2

The figure of merit is 23 — a "good" score, and the span is a tidy 1.9 m. It looks like a winner. But the chips disagree: the stall margin is a thin amber 1.15×, and Reynolds is in the red at 98.5k. That is the binding constraint. The slick AR = 11 that earned the high score also pinched the chord down to 17 cm — too short for the SD7037 to keep its boundary layer attached, so that pretty Cd0 = 0.009 from the polar is fiction at this Reynolds.

Now trade against the binding constraint, not the score. Drop AR from 11 to 8: the chord opens to 20 cm, Reynolds climbs to ~115k (out of the red), and the figure of merit settles at ~18 — still "good," and now real. That is the entire discipline of this course in one move: the honest 18 beats the fantasy 23. A defensible endurance wing is the largest, slimmest one whose chord still holds an honest Reynolds number — and you just proved yours does.

★ The capstone skill

Anyone can maximise a number. The engineer's move is to find the binding constraint — the one chip closest to red — and spend the next design cycle on that, accepting a small hit to the figure of merit to buy a wing that actually flies. Repeat until no single trade improves things without breaking another. That converged, constraint-respecting design is your endurance wing.

What you can now say

  1. A wing design is the closed loop airfoil → AR → area/loading → Reynolds → stall margin → figure of merit, iterated until the trades stop paying.
  2. Endurance is judged by the whole-wing CL1.5/CD, with CD = Cd0 + CL²/(π·AR·e) — so it climbs with AR until a constraint stops it.
  3. The figure of merit is meaningless without the constraint chips: the gold ridge lives inside the forbidden zones, and a high score on an unbuildable or out-of-regime wing is worthless.
  4. When a light goes red, trade against the binding constraint: red Reynolds → wider chord (lower AR); too-big span → higher loading or lower AR; thin stall margin → lower CL or a higher-CLmax foil.
  5. Because area cancels in Re = √(2mg/(ρ·CL·AR))/ν, the real Reynolds lever is aspect ratio, not wing size — the worked example's honest AR 8 / FoM 18 beats its fantasy AR 11 / FoM 23.
