How a Wing Really Makes Lift

Where your lift coefficient actually comes from — and the myth you have to unlearn before any of it makes sense.

⏱ ~10 minutes• 🔭 One idea, one model• Builds on F1
◎ Why this lesson

In F1 you picked target lift coefficients — CL ≈ 0.63 for best range, ≈ 1.10 for best endurance. But you can't dial a CL in directly: you set an angle of attack, and the wing converts that angle into lift. This lesson hands you the conversion, so a design CL becomes a real flying angle — and clears away the wrong mental model that would have sabotaged every later choice.

Almost everyone is taught that a wing makes lift because the top is longer than the bottom. That story is not a simplification — it is simply false, and it points your design intuition in exactly the wrong direction. Let's retire it, then build the right model in its place.

1The myth, retired

The popular explanation is the equal-transit-time story, and it fails on its own terms.3

✗ The equal-transit-time myth

“Air over the curved top has farther to travel, so it speeds up to meet the air below at the trailing edge; faster air means lower pressure, and that suction lifts the wing.” Every link in that chain breaks: the two air parcels never meet again, and the flow over the top actually moves much faster than any “catch-up” would require. A flat plate and a symmetric wing both make lift with no extra path length at all.

Here is what actually happens. The wing turns the oncoming air downward. By Newton's third law, pushing a stream of air down means the air pushes the wing up.2 Turning the flow also means curving the streamlines, and curved streamlines require a pressure drop above the wing — so Newton and Bernoulli are describing the same single event, not two rival theories. You can count the force two ways (momentum thrown down, or pressure integrated around the surface) and you get the same lift.

This isn't pedantry. The flow-turning view tells you the things that matter for endurance: lift depends on how much air you turn and how hard, which is exactly why angle of attack, camber, and speed are your levers — not some geometric path-length trick. Babinsky's smoke-tunnel photographs show the turning directly, streamline by streamline.1

2The lift curve: α in, CL out

Within the normal flying range, lift rises almost perfectly linearly with angle of attack. Three numbers define the whole lift curve:

  • Zero-lift angle (α0): a cambered airfoil makes lift even at a slightly negative angle. Ours sits at about −2°.
  • Lift-curve slope: roughly +0.11 in CL per degree for a thin airfoil — almost exactly the theoretical 2π per radian.4 (2π per radian ≈ 0.110 per degree. Finite wings are a touch shallower; we fix that in the aspect-ratio lesson.)
  • Stall: past a critical angle the flow separates from the top surface, lift collapses, and drag spikes. This caps how slow you can fly — and, as you'll see, your endurance point lives uncomfortably close to it.

Put together, those three numbers are one tidy line you can carry through the whole course:

CL = 0.11 · ( α + 2 ) Slope 0.11 per degree (≈ 2π per radian); zero-lift angle α₀ = −2°. Linear up to stall at α = 12°, where the flow separates and CL drops away. Invert it and a target lift coefficient becomes a flying angle: α = CL/0.11 − 2.

4The stall-margin payoff

Switch the explorer between the two goals and watch where they land. Best range needs only CL ≈ 0.63, which the curve delivers at a gentle α ≈ 3.7°. Best endurance needs CL ≈ 1.10, which demands α ≈ 8° — and the stall is at just 12°.

★ The payoff

Your endurance setting flies with only about 4° of stall margin, while your range setting keeps a comfortable cushion. That thin margin is precisely why an endurance design cares so much about a gentle, forgiving stall and a clean low-Reynolds airfoil — push α a little too far and the lift you were living on simply vanishes. That requirement drives the next several lessons.

What you can now say

  1. The equal-transit-time story is a myth; a wing makes lift by turning air downward (Newton), which is the same event as the pressure drop on top (Bernoulli).
  2. The lift curve is linear: CL = 0.11·(α + 2) — slope ≈ 0.11 per degree (≈ 2π per radian), zero-lift at α0 = −2°.
  3. Past the stall angle (≈ 12°) the flow separates and lift collapses, capping how slow you can fly.
  4. Inverting the curve turns a target into an angle: range CL ≈ 0.63 → α ≈ 3.7°; endurance CL ≈ 1.10 → α ≈ 8°.
  5. The endurance point sits only ~4° below stall — a thin margin that makes a gentle stall and a clean low-Re airfoil matter.
