How a Wing Really Makes Lift

Where your lift coefficient actually comes from — and the myth to unlearn first.

Why this, now
In Lesson 1 you picked target lift coefficients (CL ≈ 0.63 for best range, ≈ 1.10 for best endurance). But you can't dial in CL directly — you set angle of attack, and the wing converts it to lift. This lesson shows the conversion, so a design CL becomes a real flying angle.

The myth, retired

The most common explanation of lift is wrong, and believing it will mislead your design intuition.

✗ Equal-transit-time
“Air over the top has farther to go, so it speeds up to meet the air below at the trailing edge, and faster air means lower pressure.” The premise is simply false — the parcels never meet, and the top flow moves much faster than this story predicts.
✓ Flow turning
The wing deflects air downward. By Newton's third law, pushing air down pushes the wing up. The curved streamlines require a pressure drop above the wing — so Newton and Bernoulli describe the same event, not rival theories.

This isn't pedantry. The flow-turning view tells you the things that matter for endurance: lift depends on how much air you turn and how hard, which is why angle of attack, camber, and speed are your levers — not some geometric path-length trick. Babinsky (Cambridge) shows this with real smoke streamlines.

The lift curve: angle in, CL out

Within normal flying, lift rises almost perfectly linearly with angle of attack. Three numbers define the whole curve:

  • Zero-lift angle (α0): a cambered airfoil makes lift even at a slightly negative angle. Ours: about −2°.
  • Lift-curve slope: roughly +0.11 in CL per degree for a thin airfoil — close to the theoretical 2π per radian.2π per radian ≈ 0.110 per degree. Finite wings are a bit shallower; we'll fix that in the aspect-ratio lesson.
  • Stall: past a critical angle the flow separates, lift collapses, drag spikes. This caps how slow you can fly — and your endurance point sits closer to it than your range point.

The payoff

Best range lives near α ≈ 3.7°; best endurance near α ≈ 8° — only a few degrees below the stall around 12°. That thin margin is why endurance designs care so much about a gentle stall and a clean low-Reynolds airfoil — the subject of the next lessons.

Check yourself

From memory — the effort is the point.

In correct physics, a wing generates lift primarily because it…

Lift is reaction to deflecting air downward (Newton); the curved flow also means lower pressure on top (Bernoulli). Same event, two descriptions. The “catch-up” story is the equal-transit-time myth.

Between zero-lift angle and stall, the lift coefficient varies with angle of attack…

CL rises almost perfectly linearly with α (about +0.11 per degree) until the flow separates at stall, where it collapses.

Versus the best-range point, the best-endurance point flies at an angle that is…

Endurance needs a higher CL (~1.10), so a higher α (~8°) — much closer to the stall angle (~12°). Less stall margin is the price of minimum-power flight.

Primary source for this lesson
Babinsky, “How do wings work?” (University of Cambridge, Physics Education 2003). Five pages, real smoke-tunnel photos, and the cleanest correct account of lift you'll find. Read it once; it dissolves the myth permanently.
