Reynolds Number — Why Your Drone Is Different

A 1–2 m drone flies in a strange pocket of the atmosphere where the air feels thick and textbook airfoils quietly misbehave. This lesson names that pocket — and shows why your best endurance lever has a hidden price.

⏱ ~12 minutes• 🧮 One formula, big consequences• Builds on F3
◎ Why this lesson

In F3 you found the master endurance lever: raise aspect ratio to crush induced drag. But that lesson ended on a warning — a higher aspect ratio means a narrower chord, and a narrower chord lowers the Reynolds number the airfoil sees. This lesson turns that warning into a number you can compute, and explains why, below a critical value, a perfectly good airfoil suddenly stops working. It's the reason your drone needs airfoils most aerodynamics courses never mention.

Scale changes physics. A housefly and a Cessna both fly, but the air behaves so differently around each that what works for one is useless for the other. The single number that measures which world you're in is the Reynolds number — and your drone sits in the awkward middle, closer to the fly than the Cessna.

1What the Reynolds number is

The Reynolds number compares the two competing tendencies in any flow: inertia, which carries the air's momentum smoothly past the wing, and viscosity, the air's internal stickiness, which smears that momentum out and drags on every surface. Their ratio is one dimensionless number.1

Re = ( ρ · V · c ) / μ = V · c / ν ρ = air density, V = airspeed, c = chord (the airfoil's “size” in the flow), μ = dynamic viscosity. The two stickiness terms collapse into one constant, the kinematic viscosity ν = μ/ρ ≈ 1.5×10⁻⁵ m²/s for air at sea level — so for us, Re = V·c / ν. Bigger, faster wings → high Re (inertia wins). Tiny, slow wings → low Re (viscosity wins).

Two consequences fall straight out of that formula, and you'll feel both for the rest of the course. First, Reynolds number is set by chord, not span — so a long, slender wing of the same area flies at a lower Re than a stubby one. Second, flying slower (which endurance wants, from F1) also lowers Re. Endurance design keeps nudging you toward the low-Reynolds danger zone from two directions at once.

2The regime ladder — and where you sit

Run the numbers for different fliers and they sort into a ladder spanning four orders of magnitude. Each rung is a genuinely different aerodynamic world.2

This is the whole point of the lesson. Almost every airfoil chart, rule of thumb, and intuition in a standard aerodynamics text was measured up on that bottom rung, at Re in the millions. Your drone flies one or two rungs down, where those same airfoils can lose a third of their performance — or more.

3Why low Reynolds is hard

The trouble lives in the boundary layer — the thin sheet of slowed air clinging to the wing's surface. At high Re this layer trips quickly into a turbulent state, which is energetic and sticks to the surface well. At low Re it stays laminar: smooth, orderly, and weak. A laminar layer has little energy to fight the rising pressure over the rear of the airfoil, so it lets go — it separates from the surface.1

✗ The laminar separation bubble

Often the separated flow trips to turbulent in mid-air and reattaches a little further back, trapping a stagnant laminar separation bubble under it. That bubble is dead weight: it thickens the effective shape, raises drag, lowers the maximum lift coefficient, and cuts the airfoil's best L/D — exactly the figure your endurance number lives on. Drop Re far enough and the bubble bursts without reattaching, and the airfoil simply gives up.3

Climb back above the critical range and the picture flips: the boundary layer transitions early, stays attached, the bubble shrinks and vanishes, and the airfoil “wakes up” to something close to its high-Reynolds promise. So airfoil performance isn't flat across Reynolds number — it rises with Re, with a knee around 100k–150k and a steep cliff below ~80k. The explorer below lets you walk that curve.

5The catch: aspect ratio isn't free

Now the two halves of the course collide. F3 told you to stretch the wing — high aspect ratio is the single biggest lever on induced drag. But aspect ratio is span²/area, and for a fixed area, more span means a thinner chord. Thinner chord means lower Reynolds number, which means a sicker airfoil. You cannot freely have both.

★ The key tension

Raising aspect ratio shrinks the chord, which lowers the Reynolds number your airfoil sees — so AR is not a free lunch. Push aspect ratio too far on a small drone and you win on induced drag but lose more on the airfoil, as its separation bubble grows and its L/D collapses. The endurance-optimal wing is a negotiated settlement between these two pulls, and you can't even start the negotiation until you can compute the Reynolds number — which you now can.

What you can now say

  1. Re = V·c/ν is the ratio of inertial to viscous forces; for air ν ≈ 1.5×10⁻⁵ m²/s. It rises with speed and chord, and falls when either drops.
  2. Fliers sort onto a four-rung Reynolds ladder; your 1–2 m drone lives at roughly 100k–400k — the tricky low-Re transition, not the textbook millions.
  3. At low Re the laminar boundary layer separates into a laminar separation bubble that raises drag, lowers CLmax, and cuts max L/D.
  4. Airfoil performance rises with Re: healthy above ~150k, marginal from ~80k–150k, and bubble-dominated and risky below ~80k.
  5. Aspect ratio is not free: more AR → narrower chord → lower Re. The endurance lever and the Reynolds penalty pull against each other.
