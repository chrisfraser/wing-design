The Wing as a Beam: Spars & Loads

Every choice the aerodynamics arc made — long span, thin section — sends a bill to the structure. This lesson reads that bill and sizes the spar that pays it.

⏱ ~14 minutes• 🧮 First-order structures math• Builds on D1 · F3
◎ Why this lesson

The aero arc kept pushing the same way. F3 wanted high aspect ratio to cut induced drag; F4 and the low-Reynolds airfoils wanted thin sections; the D1 capstone landed on a long, slender, thin-foiled endurance wing. Aerodynamically: ideal. Structurally: those are the two worst things you can ask of a beam. A longer span multiplies the bending load, and a thinner section gives you less depth to resist it. This lesson turns that tension into numbers — and hands you the equation that sizes the spar so the wing you designed can actually survive a pull-up.

A wing is not just an aerofoil — it is a cantilever beam, built into the fuselage at one end and carrying the whole aircraft's weight along its length. When lift exceeds weight in a turn or a gust, that beam tries to fold upward at the root. Everything in wing structure is about stopping it.

1A wing is a cantilever beam

Picture the wing held only at the root, with lift spread along the span pushing it up. That is the textbook definition of a cantilever: a beam supported at one end only. The lift outboard of any point has to be carried across that point and back to the root — so the further inboard you look, the more lift is stacked outboard of you trying to bend the wing. Bending is therefore greatest at the root and falls to zero at the tip, where there is nothing outboard left to lift.1

The thing that measures "how hard the beam is being bent" at a station is the bending moment M — force times the distance it acts through. The root carries the maximum, so the root is where a wing breaks. Size the spar for the root and the rest of the span is along for the ride.

2The root bending moment

In steady level flight lift equals weight, W = m·g. But wings are not sized for steady flight — they are sized for the worst pull-up or gust, captured by the load factor n (the "g's" the airframe pulls). Total lift in an n-g manoeuvre is n·W. For a clean elliptical lift distribution, each half-wing's load acts at its centroid, 4L/(3π) out from the root (L = half-span). Multiply load by lever arm and the root bending moment falls out:

Mroot = n · W · b / (3π) ≈ 0.106 · n · W · b W = m · g b = full span (m), n = load factor, W = weight (N). The whole point: Mroot ∝ n·W·b — bending climbs in direct proportion to span. A uniform lift load would give n·W·b/8 = 0.125·n·W·b; an elliptical load packs more lift inboard, so its root moment is a touch lower at 0.106·n·W·b.
⚖ How big is n? Where the load factor comes from

You do not invent the load factor — the certification rules set it. The FAA's 14 CFR 23.337 gives limit manoeuvring load factors of 3.8 g (Normal), 4.4 g (Utility) and 6.0 g (Acrobatic), each multiplied by a 1.5 safety factor to get the ultimate load the structure must not break under. Model sailplanes and endurance UAVs are commonly built to about 5 g. Our explorer defaults to n = 4 — a sane design pull-up for a gentle endurance machine.3

3How a spar carries it: caps in a couple

A spar resists bending the way a steel I-beam does: with material pushed as far from the neutral axis as possible. The two spar caps — top and bottom flanges — do almost all the work, carrying the bending as a pure axial couple: the top cap in compression, the bottom cap in tension. The shear web and skin between them carry shear and torsion (we only mention those here). What sets the leverage of that couple is the spar height h — how far apart the caps sit — and that is fixed by how thick the aerofoil is:

h ≈ 0.8 · (t/c) · croot σ = Mroot / (A · h) ⇒ A = Mroot / (σallow · h) t/c = airfoil thickness ratio, croot = root chord. The beam relation σ = M·c/I, idealising the spar as two caps of area A separated by h (so I ≈ A·h²/2 and c = h/2), collapses to σ = M/(A·h).2 Rearranged, the required area of each cap is A = Mroot/(σallow·h). Smaller h (a thinner section) ⇒ bigger A. That is the structural cost of a thin airfoil, written as an equation.

Read the last equation slowly, because it contains the whole lesson. Cap area A goes up when the moment Mroot goes up (longer span, more g, more weight) and up again when the height h goes down (a thinner section). The two things the aerodynamics begged for — more span and a thinner foil — both push cap area, and therefore spar weight, the wrong way.

Numbers make it concrete. Take a 2 kg wing of 2 m span pulling n = 4 g, on a 10% section with a 0.18 m root chord:

W = m·g = 2 × 9.81 = 19.6 N Mroot = n·W·b/(3π) = 4 × 19.6 × 2 / (3π) = 16.6 N·m h = 0.8 × 0.10 × 0.18 = 0.0144 m = 14.4 mm A = Mroot/(σ·h): carbon → 2.3 mm² · balsa → 64 mm² (per cap) Same moment, same height — only the material changed. Balsa's working stress is ~28× lower than carbon's, so each balsa cap needs ~28× the area to hold the same load. This is why the spar material choice (next lesson, B2) is so consequential.

The spar mass is a first-order, bending-only estimate (two caps tapering to the tips). Real spars add a shear web, root joiners and a buckling margin, and run heavier — treat this as the irreducible floor, not the finished weight.

5The structural bill for the aerodynamics

Put the two effects side by side. Stretching the span from 2 m to 3 m raises Mroot by 50% — and because the spar mass scales with that moment and the longer length it runs over, the cap weight climbs faster still. Independently, thinning the foil from 12% to 9% shrinks the spar height h by a quarter, and cap area A = M/(σ·h) grows by a third for the very same load. Neither knob touches the aerodynamics in this lesson, yet both were turned hard by the aero arc.

★ The structural price of a good wing

Because Mroot ∝ n·W·b, doubling the span doubles the root bending — high aspect ratio buys aerodynamic efficiency on the structure's credit card. And because A = Mroot/(σ·h) with h ∝ (t/c)·croot, a thinner low-Reynolds section gives the caps less height to work with, so they must get heavier to hold the same moment. The long, slender, thin-foiled wing that F3, F4 and D1 converged on is exactly the wing that is hardest to build light. The spar is where that bill comes due — and choosing the right material to pay it (carbon over balsa) is the subject of B2.

What you can now say

  1. A wing is a cantilever beam built in at the root, so its bending moment is maximum at the root and zero at the tip.
  2. The root bending moment is Mroot = n·W·b/(3π) ≈ 0.106·n·W·b for an elliptical load — it scales directly with load factor, weight and span.
  3. The spar caps carry bending as a couple (top compression, bottom tension); the web and skin carry shear and torsion.
  4. Each cap needs area A = Mroot/(σallow·h), with spar height h ≈ 0.8·(t/c)·croot — a thinner section means smaller h and so more cap area.
  5. High aspect ratio (longer b) and thin low-Re sections (smaller h) both drive spar weight up — the structural bill for the aerodynamic choices of F3, F4 and D1.
