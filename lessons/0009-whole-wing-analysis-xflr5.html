Whole-Wing Analysis in XFLR5

Your airfoil polars describe a slice of wing one millimetre thick. A real wing has ends — and those ends change everything. Here we go from 2-D to 3-D.

⏱ ~14 minutes• 🧮 One clean formula, computed live• Builds on F3 · T1 · T2
◎ Why this lesson

In T2 you turned aspect ratio and area into a real span and chord; in T1 you generated honest 2-D polars for your airfoil. But a 2-D polar is a lie of omission — it assumes the wing is infinitely long. The moment your wing has tips, it sheds vortices, makes induced drag, and loses some lift-curve slope. This lesson shows you how the planform controls that loss, and how XFLR5 computes the polar of the whole wing — the real CL1.5/CD you'll actually fly.

An airfoil is a cross-section. A wing is a finite object with two ends, and air does not politely flow straight back over those ends — it spills from the high-pressure belly around the tip to the low-pressure top, rolling up into a pair of trailing vortices. Those vortices are the price of being three-dimensional, and they are why your wing always performs a little worse than its airfoil promised.

1Why a 3-D wing isn't its 2-D section

Back in F3 you met induced drag: the drag a finite wing pays to make lift, born from the tip vortices. Those same vortices induce a gentle downwash over the whole span, which tilts the local airflow downward. The wing therefore meets the air at a smaller effective angle than the one you set — so it makes less lift per degree than the 2-D section did. Two consequences follow, and both hurt:

  • Extra drag. The lift vector tilts back with the airflow, leaving a rearward component — induced drag — that simply doesn't exist in 2-D.
  • A shallower lift-curve slope. The finite wing needs a larger geometric angle to reach the same CL. The 2-D slope of ~0.11 per degree softens toward ~0.08–0.09 per degree on a typical model wing.

The induced-drag coefficient is the single most important equation in this lesson — you already saw it in F3, and now we're going to learn what sets that mysterious e:4

CDi = CL² / ( π · AR · e ) AR = aspect ratio, e = span efficiency factor (≤ 1). Higher AR and higher e both cut induced drag. The e here is the inviscid, wing-alone span efficiency set by planform shape — not the whole-aircraft Oswald factor (more on that below).

And the shallower slope is real, predictable physics from lifting-line theory — the finite-wing lift-curve slope a falls below the 2-D value a0:3

a = a0 / ( 1 + a0 / ( π · AR · e ) ) With a0 ≈ 2π per radian and AR = 8, e ≈ 0.95: the slope drops from 0.110 to about 0.087 per degree. A higher aspect ratio recovers most of it — another reason endurance wings run long and slender.

2The lift distribution, and the magic of elliptical

Picture the lift spread across the span — heavy in the middle, fading to zero at each tip. The shape of that spanwise lift distribution is what sets e. There is one shape that is provably optimal: the elliptical distribution, which produces uniform downwash across the span and gives the theoretical minimum induced drag — e = 1.0.3 Every other shape wastes a little.

A plain rectangular wing carries too much load out near the tips, where it does the most vortex-making harm. It lands around e ≈ 0.93 (at AR 8) — roughly 7% more induced drag than ideal. The fix is almost embarrassingly cheap: taper the wing, narrowing the chord toward the tip. A moderate taper ratio λ = ctip/croot ≈ 0.4 pulls the load distribution almost onto the elliptical curve and recovers e ≈ 0.98–0.99 — without the manufacturing nightmare of building a true ellipse.

Where does that number come from? We compute e live from the verified Hörner / Niță–Scholz result,5 which turns the planform taper ratio straight into a span-efficiency factor:

e = 1 / ( 1 + δ ) δ = AR · f(λ) f(λ) = 0.0524 λ⁴ − 0.1500 λ³ + 0.1659 λ² − 0.0706 λ + 0.0119 λ = taper ratio (tip chord ÷ root chord). The penalty factor f(λ) is smallest near λ ≈ 0.357 — but the minimum is broad and shallow, so anything from λ ≈ 0.3 to 0.5 sits within ~1% of ideal. A true ellipse drives f → 0, hence e = 1.
⚠ Don't confuse e with the Oswald factor

The e we compute here is the inviscid, wing-alone span efficiency — it accounts only for the planform's lift distribution. The whole-aircraft Oswald efficiency factor you'll see quoted as ~0.7–0.85 is a different, larger bookkeeping number: it also folds in viscous drag-due-to-lift, the fuselage, and other interference. XFLR5 reports the wing-alone figure; keep the two straight so you don't double-count the penalty.

4The XFLR5 workflow, step by step

XFLR5 (Deperrois) is a free GUI that wraps XFOIL and adds a 3-D solver: lifting-line theory (LLT) and the vortex-lattice method (VLM). You feed it the airfoil polars you generated in T1, describe your planform, and it returns the whole-wing polar — including induced drag, span efficiency, and the spanwise CL distribution.1

  1. Import your airfoil's 2-D polars (from T1)
    In Direct Foil Design, load your .dat coordinates. Then in XFoil Direct Analysis, run a batch sweep of viscous polars across a range of Reynolds numbers that brackets your wing's operating Re (recall T2 — chord sets Re). XFLR5 will interpolate between these at each spanwise station.
  2. Define the wing in the Plane / Wing editor
    Switch to Wing and Plane Design. Set the span, root chord and tip chord — their ratio is your taper λ. Confirm the resulting area and aspect ratio match the numbers you sized in T2. Assign your imported foil to the sections.
  3. Choose an analysis: LLT or VLM
    For a straight, unswept, moderate-AR wing, LLT is fast and accurate and reports span efficiency directly. Use VLM (or panels) when you add sweep, dihedral, or a full plane where LLT's assumptions break down.
  4. Run the analysis across an α sweep
    Define a sequence of angles of attack and run. XFLR5 solves the lift distribution at each α, blending in the viscous 2-D drag from your imported polars to get total CD = profile + induced.
  5. Read the whole-wing polar and the endurance number
    Plot CL–CD, CL–α, and — the one you care about — CL1.5/CD versus CL. Check the span efficiency and the spanwise CL plot to confirm the tips aren't stalling first. This is your wing's real performance — the airfoil's promise, taxed by three dimensions.
★ The through-line

Airfoil polars (T1) describe a 2-D slice. Planform (T2) and taper (this lesson) decide how that slice is distributed over a finite span. XFLR5 stitches them together into one CL1.5/CD for the whole wing — the number that finally answers "how long will it stay up?". That's exactly what D1 will let you synthesise end-to-end.

What you can now say

  1. A finite wing sheds tip vortices, which create induced drag and a shallower lift-curve slope than the 2-D section.
  2. Induced drag is CDi = CL²/(π·AR·e) — high AR and high e both cut it.
  3. The elliptical lift distribution is optimal (e = 1); a rectangular wing wastes ~7% (e ≈ 0.93).
  4. A moderate taper λ ≈ 0.4 recovers e ≈ 0.98–0.99 cheaply — the broad optimum sits near λ ≈ 0.357.
  5. XFLR5 imports your T1 airfoil polars, takes your planform, runs LLT/VLM, and returns the whole-wing polar and its real CL1.5/CD.
